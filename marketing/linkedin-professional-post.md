**Body:**

Here's the engineering leader's dilemma: Your backlog is growing faster than your hiring pipeline.

You need more output, but hiring is slow. Onboarding is expensive. Building deep expertise in your codebase takes months.

Last month, I stopped treating AI as a tool. I started treating it as a *team member*.

Instead of one really good assistant, I structured multiple AI agents into specialized roles:

✓ A lead who decomposes complex tasks into parallel workstreams
✓ A code expert who handles implementation and testing
✓ A security specialist who audits for vulnerabilities
✓ A documentation lead who captures knowledge automatically
✓ An infrastructure engineer who manages deployments

Each agent has read your codebase. They know your patterns. They make decisions aligned with your architecture. And they work 24/7.

Here's what six weeks looked like:

- **Week 1:** "Okay, this is interesting but will probably break things" (overhead while agents learned)
- **Week 2-3:** "Wait, these PRs are actually good" (agents started understanding patterns)
- **Week 4-6:** "Why am I still reviewing? They're doing better work than I'm directing" (knowledge compounding)

The result? My engineering velocity *doubled* while I spent less time in code review and more time on architectural decisions.

**The business case:**
- You can't hire 7 senior engineers in 6 weeks
- You can structure AI agents in 1 day
- Your best engineers focus on judgment calls, not code implementation
- Knowledge compounds instead of walking out the door when someone quits
- You maintain control: humans still approve security changes and architecture decisions

This isn't about replacing engineers. It's about *augmenting* your existing team's capacity so your best people focus on what only humans can do.

If you're an engineering leader fighting hiring delays or trying to ship faster with your current team, this might be worth a 20-minute read.
