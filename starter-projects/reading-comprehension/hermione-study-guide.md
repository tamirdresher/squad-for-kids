## 1. Finding the Main Idea

> *"I always highlight the topic sentence first. It's just logical!"* — Hermione 🖊️

The **main idea** is what the whole passage is mostly about. It is **NOT** a small detail — it is the big umbrella that all the other sentences stand under.

### 🔑 Hermione's 3-Step Strategy

| Step | What to do | Hermione's tip |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **Step 1** | Read the **first** and **last** paragraphs carefully | "Authors usually state the main idea at the very beginning — or sometimes they save it for a dramatic conclusion!" |
| **Step 2** | Ask yourself: *"What is almost every sentence talking about?"* | "If it keeps coming up, it's probably important." |
| **Step 3** | Write it in your own words in **one sentence** | "No, really — one sentence. I know it's hard. I've practised!" |

### 📌 Example

**Passage:**
> *Dogs have been living with humans for thousands of years. They help farmers herd animals, assist police officers in searches, and guide people with disabilities. In addition, dogs provide companionship and reduce feelings of loneliness. Scientists have found that petting a dog lowers a person's stress levels. It is clear that dogs play many important roles in human life.*

**Main idea:** Dogs play many important roles in human life.

**Why?** Every sentence gives an example of dogs helping humans. The last sentence sums it all up — that is a classic main-idea placement! ✅

### ⚡ Quick-Check Questions to Ask Yourself
- What does the author keep coming back to?
- Which sentence could be the title of this passage?
- If I removed this sentence, would the passage still make sense? *(If yes → it's a detail, not the main idea)*

## 2. Finding Specific Details

> *"I annotate EVERYTHING. Every. Single. Page. Even Neville can do this — and he did!"* — Hermione 📝

Specific detail questions ask you to find **one precise piece of information** from the passage. The answer is always **in the text** — you just need to find it.

### 🔑 Hermione's Detail-Finding Method

**Before you read:**
- 📖 Skim the questions *first* so you know what to hunt for
- 🖊️ Have a pencil ready — underlining is your best friend

**While you read:**
- Underline **names, numbers, dates, and places** automatically
- Circle **cause-and-effect signal words**: *because, as a result, therefore, so that*
- Put a star ⭐ next to any sentence that answers one of the questions

**When you answer:**
- Go **back** to the passage — never rely on memory alone
- Find the **exact sentence** and point your finger at it
- Make sure your answer matches the text **word for word** (or very close to it)

### 📌 Common Question Phrases and Where to Look

| Question says... | Where to look |
|-----------------|---------------|
| "According to the passage..." | Anywhere — scan for the keyword |
| "In the second paragraph..." | Go straight to paragraph 2 |
| "The author states that..." | Look for a direct statement |
| "Which detail supports..." | Find the evidence sentence |
| "Based on the passage, why..." | Look for *because* or *so* |

### ⚡ Hermione's Golden Rule
> *"If you can't point to a specific line in the passage, the answer is wrong. It must be IN THE TEXT!"*

## 3. Multiple Choice Strategies

> *"Multiple choice is just a logic puzzle. Treat it like a Potions exam — eliminate what you know is wrong first."* — Hermione ⚗️

### 🔑 The Elimination Method (Hermione's Favourite!)

```
Step 1 → Read the question carefully (underline the KEY word)
Step 2 → Cover the answers and predict your own answer
Step 3 → Eliminate answers that are clearly WRONG
Step 4 → Compare the remaining answers — pick the BEST one
Step 5 → Go back to the passage to confirm ✅
```

### 🚫 Answer Types to Eliminate Immediately

| Type | Why it's wrong | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|
| **Too extreme** | Uses words like *always, never, all, none* | "Dogs are NEVER helpful" |
| **Out of scope** | True, but not in the passage | A fact you know from science class |
| **Opposite** | Contradicts the passage directly | Passage says "warm" → answer says "cold" |
| **Half-right / half-wrong** | First part is correct, second part isn't | Read the WHOLE answer carefully! |
| **Too narrow** | Only covers one small detail, not the main idea | For main-idea questions |

### 📌 Hermione's Secret Weapons

**🪄 For "main idea" questions:**
- Eliminate answers about small details
- The correct answer covers the WHOLE passage

**🪄 For "what does the word mean" questions:**
- Re-read the sentence with each answer choice substituted in
- Pick the one that makes the most sense in context

**🪄 For "the author's purpose" questions:**
- *Inform* = giving facts and explanations
- *Persuade* = trying to change your opinion
- *Entertain* = telling a story or making you laugh

**🪄 If you're completely stuck:**
- Eliminate the two most obviously wrong answers
- Between the remaining two, choose the one that uses words from the passage
- Never leave a blank — there's no penalty for guessing!

## 4. Vocabulary Tips (Unit 5)

> *"I read the dictionary for fun, but I understand not everyone does that. These strategies will help you figure out any word!"* — Hermione 📖

### 🔑 Strategy 1: Context Clues

The **context** — the sentences around the unfamiliar word — often gives you clues to its meaning.

**Types of context clues:**

| Clue Type | Signal Words | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Definition** | *is, means, refers to, which is* | "The habitat, or the place where an animal lives, must provide food and shelter." |
| **Synonym** | *also called, or, in other words* | "She was obstinate — in other words, she stubbornly refused to change her mind." |
| **Antonym** | *but, however, unlike, although* | "Unlike her timid brother, Maya was bold and fearless." |
| **Example** | *such as, for example, including* | "Various predators, such as wolves, eagles, and sharks, hunt other animals." |
| **Inference** | No signal word — use logic! | "The exhausted hikers collapsed onto their sleeping bags." → *exhausted* must mean very tired |

### 🔑 Strategy 2: Word Parts (Roots, Prefixes, Suffixes)

| Part | Meaning | Examples |
|------|---------|---------|
| **pre-** | before | *pre*view, *pre*historic, *pre*dict |
| **re-** | again | *re*read, *re*write, *re*turn |
| **un-** | not | *un*happy, *un*clear, *un*usual |
| **-ful** | full of | help*ful*, wonder*ful*, power*ful* |
| **-less** | without | hope*less*, speech*less*, care*less* |
| **-tion / -sion** | the act of | informa*tion*, explo*sion*, connec*tion* |
| **dict** | say/speak | pre*dict*, contra*dict*, *dict*ionary |
| **port** | carry | trans*port*, im*port*, *port*able |
| **vis / vid** | see | *vis*ible, tele*vision*, e*vidence* |

### 🔑 Strategy 3: Hermione's 4-Question Method

When you see an unfamiliar word:
1. **What part of speech is it?** (noun, verb, adjective, adverb)
2. **What does the sentence say around it?**
3. **Does it have any word parts I recognise?**
4. **Which answer choice makes the sentence make sense?**

> *"Even if you don't know a word, you can almost always figure it out. I promise!"* ✨

## Practice Passage A

### 📜 *The Great Library of Alexandria*

*Read the passage below, then answer the questions.*

---

The ancient Library of Alexandria, located in Egypt, was once the greatest collection of knowledge in the entire world. Built around 300 BCE, it held hundreds of thousands of scrolls on subjects ranging from astronomy and medicine to philosophy and poetry. Scholars from across the Mediterranean Sea travelled long distances just to study its remarkable collection.

The library was more than just a storehouse of books. It was also a research centre, called the Mouseion, where thinkers lived and worked together. Scientists performed experiments, mathematicians solved equations, and poets composed verses — all under one roof. The scholar Eratosthenes even calculated the circumference of the Earth there with impressive accuracy, using only a stick and the position of the sun.

Unfortunately, the library did not survive. Historians disagree about exactly what happened, but most believe it declined gradually over several centuries due to neglect and a series of destructive events, including fires and political conflicts. By the time of the Arab conquest of Egypt in 641 CE, little or nothing of the original library remained.

Today, the Bibliotheca Alexandrina — a modern library built near the same site — honours the legacy of the ancient one. It serves millions of visitors each year and reminds us that the pursuit of knowledge is always worth preserving.

## Practice Passage B

### 🌊 *The Hidden Ocean Beneath the Ice*

*Read the passage below, then answer the questions.*

---

Europa, one of Jupiter's moons, may seem like a frozen and lifeless world. Its surface is covered in a thick layer of ice that stretches across the entire moon. However, scientists believe something extraordinary lies beneath: a vast liquid ocean, possibly two to three times larger than all of Earth's oceans combined.

This hidden ocean stays liquid despite Europa's distance from the sun because of a process called tidal heating. Jupiter's powerful gravity constantly squeezes and stretches Europa as it orbits, generating heat inside the moon. This internal warmth prevents the water beneath the ice from freezing solid.

What excites scientists most is the possibility that this ocean could harbour life. On Earth, life thrives in the darkest, coldest parts of the deep ocean around hydrothermal vents — cracks in the ocean floor that release heat and minerals. Scientists believe similar vents might exist on Europa's ocean floor, providing the energy and nutrients that simple organisms would need to survive.

NASA has planned a mission called Europa Clipper, which will fly past the moon dozens of times to study its ice shell and ocean. If evidence of life — even microscopic life — were discovered there, it would be one of the most significant scientific findings in all of human history.

---

### Questions

**1.** What is the main idea of this passage?

- A) Jupiter has many interesting moons
- B) Europa's hidden ocean makes it a promising place to search for life
- C) NASA is planning many exciting space missions
- D) Tidal heating is a complicated scientific process

---

**2.** According to the passage, why does Europa's ocean stay liquid?

- A) Europa is close enough to the sun to stay warm
- B) The ocean is protected by a thick layer of rock
- C) Tidal heating from Jupiter's gravity generates warmth inside the moon
- D) Underwater volcanoes keep the water temperature high

---

**3.** Based on the context, what does the word **"harbour"** most likely mean in this sentence: *"this ocean could harbour life"*?

- A) Block or prevent
- B) Contain or provide shelter for
- C) Destroy completely
- D) Study carefully

---

**4.** Which piece of evidence does the author use to support the idea that life could exist on Europa?

- A) Europa is larger than Earth's moon
- B) The Europa Clipper mission has already launched

## Before-the-Test Checklist

> *"Preparation is not cheating — it is wisdom."* — Hermione Granger 🦁

- [ ] 📖 Re-read your study notes once — don't cram, just review
- [ ] 🖊️ Make sure you have a pencil (for annotating the passage)

- [ ] 🍳 Eat breakfast — your brain needs fuel

### 📋 During the Test

- [ ] 📖 **Read the questions FIRST** before reading the passage

- [ ] ⭐ **Star** sentences that answer a question
